At any given second, there are over 10,000 commercial aircraft in the sky. This map tracks the chaotic, yet precise, web of modern aviation.

If you were to paint a line on the sky for every plane that flew in the last 24 hours, the sun would be blotted out over Europe and North America. The image above is a composite of billions of ADS-B transponder signals, creating a glowing spiderweb of human movement.

This is not just a map of travel; it is a map of speed. While shipping routes represent volume (heavy, slow, cheap), air traffic represents value (light, fast, expensive). The goods moving on these lines—pharmaceuticals, electronics, fresh flowers—account for less than 1% of trade volume by weight, but over 35% by value.

The "Great Circle" Deception

Look at the flight paths between New York and London. On a flat map, they appear to curve dramatically northward, almost touching Greenland. This is not a detour; it is a straight line.

Because the Earth is a sphere, the shortest distance between two points is a "Great Circle" arc. Airlines do not fly straight lines on a map; they fly geodesics on a globe to save fuel.

Visual Identification

In the World Pulse Game, this map is notoriously difficult because it resembles Internet Cables and Satellite Orbits.

The Tell: Look for the "Void over Tibet."
There is a massive dark hole over the Himalayas where almost no planes fly. This is not because of politics, but physics. If a cabin depressurizes over the Tibetan Plateau, planes must descend to 10,000 feet to allow passengers to breathe. In Tibet, the ground is often higher than 10,000 feet. Therefore, airlines avoid the region entirely to ensure they have a safe descent altitude.

ETOPS Ratings

Why do twin-engine planes cross the ocean? Modern engines are so reliable they are certified for "ETOPS" (Extended-range Twin-engine Operational Performance Standards), allowing them to fly up to 370 minutes away from the nearest airport.

Log 002

The Jet Stream

Flights from New York to London are often an hour faster than the return trip. This is because pilots ride the Jet Stream—a river of air moving east at up to 250 mph. Flying against it is like swimming upstream.

Log 003

Ghost Freighters

Many "passenger" planes flying at night are actually filled with cargo. The belly of a Boeing 777 carries tons of mail and freight. Your Amazon package often flies business class under your feet.

Log 004

The 5G Confusion

This map is distinct from Cellular Coverage. While planes have Wi-Fi, they connect to satellites or ground towers pointed upwards. A cellular map shows the ground; an aviation map shows the corridors above it.

About The Dataset

This visualization is aggregated from ADS-B (Automatic Dependent Surveillance–Broadcast) data. Every modern commercial aircraft broadcasts its position, altitude, and velocity twice per second to ground stations and satellites.

By compiling a year's worth of this data, we reveal the "Highways of the Sky." Notice how the North Atlantic and the corridor between Europe and Asia are essentially solid blocks of traffic, while the Southern Hemisphere remains comparatively sparse.

QuizRealm Intelligence uses this data to illustrate global connectivity and the physical limitations of high-altitude travel.
