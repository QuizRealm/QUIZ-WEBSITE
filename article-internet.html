Briefing #003 Digital Infrastructure

The Glass
Ocean.

There is no cloud. There is only thin glass wire resting on the ocean floor. This is the fragile nervous system of our species.

We tend to think of the internet as a wireless, ethereal "cloud." This is a convenient fiction. The reality is shockingly physical: 99% of all international data traffic—every email, bank transfer, and TikTok video—travels through tubes the width of a garden hose submerged in the deep ocean.

The map above visualizes these Submarine Fiber Optic Cables. They are the most critical, expensive, and vulnerable infrastructure project in human history. Without them, the global economy ceases to function within seconds. When a cable is cut, bank trades stall, video calls freeze, and entire nations can feel suddenly "far away" again.

The Internet Has a Shape

When you play the World Pulse Game, this dataset is often mistaken for shipping routes or flight paths. At a distance, the patterns look similar: bright arcs hugging coastlines, dense knots where continents pinch together. But this map is not about ships or planes—it is about latency, bandwidth, and geopolitical leverage.

Each glowing line represents strands of hair-thin glass that can carry terabits of information per second. Yet their physical reality is almost comically fragile: they lie unguarded on the seabed, sometimes buried a few centimeters deep, sometimes simply resting on mud.

Technical Readout: The Physical Layer

Composition

Hair-thin glass fibers encased in steel wire, copper sheathing, waterproofing layers and polyethylene insulation.

Transmission Speed

Up to 224 Terabits per second (Tbps) on modern transatlantic systems, using dense wavelength-division multiplexing.

Primary Threats

Fishing trawlers, dragging ship anchors, undersea landslides, and targeted sabotage.

The Choke Points

The geometry of the continents forces these cables into narrow, highly congested corridors. Look at the map: the Strait of Malacca, the Suez Canal, the Mediterranean, and the coasts of New York and Cornwall are massive bottlenecks. A single localized event in these areas—natural or man-made—can sever connectivity for entire regions.

Control of these "landing stations" is the new Great Game of geopolitics. They are where fibers emerge from the sea and enter terrestrial networks. Who owns the building, the switches, the maintenance contracts? Increasingly, the answer is not just telecom companies, but also cloud giants who now design, finance and operate their own cables to reduce dependency on third parties.

Strategic Corridors

  • Transatlantic links tying North America to Western Europe.
  • Transpacific arcs between the US West Coast and East Asia.
  • Chokepoints like the Red Sea and Mediterranean for Euro–Asia traffic.

Risk Profiles

  • Shallow coastal waters: high risk from anchors and trawlers.
  • Seismically active zones: risk from underwater landslides.
  • Politically contested coastlines: risk from deliberate interference.

For analysts inside The World Pulse syllabus, these choke points are not just trivia—they are training data. Recognizing where cables converge teaches you where digital power is most concentrated and where it is easiest to disrupt.

Why Satellites Don't Replace Cables

If undersea cables are so vulnerable, why not move everything to space? The answer is a mix of physics, economics, and scale.

  • Bandwidth: A single modern cable can move more data than an entire fleet of satellites.
  • Latency: Even with fiber’s slower speed of light in glass, a direct undersea path beats bouncing signals to orbit and back.
  • Cost per bit: Once installed, fiber is incredibly cheap to operate compared to launching and maintaining hardware in orbit.

Result: satellites are excellent for coverage and redundancy, but the backbone of the internet will remain underwater glass for the foreseeable future. In the Arcade, this distinction becomes a puzzle about capacity, not just coverage.

Additional Data Points

Latency Physics

Light travels about 31% slower through glass fiber than it does through a vacuum. Despite this penalty, a direct fiber path still outperforms satellite constellations for real-time applications like trading and gaming.

The Primary Threat

A popular myth blames sharks for attacking cables. In reality, most breaks are caused by human activity in shallow waters: fishing trawlers scraping the seabed and mis-dropped ship anchors that snag the lines.

About The Data Source

The submarine cable data visualized here is aggregated from public telecommunications registries and industry oversight bodies like TeleGeography. It represents active and planned fiber optic systems laid on the ocean floor at the time of capture.

Unlike satellite maps which show broad coverage areas, this map shows precise point-to-point physical connections. The visual similarity to ancient maritime trade routes is not coincidental; digital infrastructure often follows the path of least resistance established by centuries of shipping.
