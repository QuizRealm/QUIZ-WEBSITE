Briefing #003 Digital Infrastructure

The Glass
Ocean.

There is no cloud. There is only thin glass wire resting on the ocean floor. This is the fragile nervous system of our species.

We tend to think of the internet as a wireless, ethereal "cloud." This is a convenient fiction. The reality is shockingly physical: 99% of all international data traffic—every email, bank transfer, and TikTok video—travels through tubes the width of a garden hose submerged in the deep ocean.

The map above visualizes these Submarine Fiber Optic Cables. They are the most critical, expensive, and vulnerable infrastructure project in human history. Without them, the global economy ceases to function within seconds.

Technical Readout: The Physical Layer

Composition

Hair-thin glass fibers encased in steel wire, copper sheathing, and polyethylene insulation.

Transmission Speed

Up to 224 Terabits per second (Tbps) on modern transatlantic systems.

Primary Threats

Fishing Trawlers, Ship Anchors, Seismic Activity.

The Choke Points

The geometry of the continents forces these cables into narrow, highly congested corridors. Look at the map: the Strait of Malacca, the Suez Canal, and the coasts of New York and Cornwall are massive bottlenecks. A single localized event in these areas—natural or man-made—can sever connectivity for entire continents.

Control of these "landing stations" is the new Great Game of geopolitics. It is why tech giants like Google and Meta have stopped renting bandwidth and started laying their own private oceans of glass.

Additional Data Points

Latency Physics

Light travels 31% slower through glass fiber than it does through a vacuum. Despite this physics penalty, submarine cables still vastly outperform satellite networks in total bandwidth capacity and reliability.

The Primary Threat

A common myth is that sharks bite internet cables. The reality is mundane: 60% of all cable failures are caused by fishing trawlers and dragging ship anchors. Geological events account for only 12%.

About The Data Source

The submarine cable data visualized here is aggregated from public telecommunications registries and industry oversight bodies like TeleGeography. It represents active and planned fiber optic systems laid on the ocean floor.

Unlike satellite maps which show broad coverage areas, this map shows precise point-to-point physical connections. The visual similarity to ancient trade routes is not coincidental; digital infrastructure often follows the path of least resistance established by maritime history.
